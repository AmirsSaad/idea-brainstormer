Drone Video Relay Add-On with Beam-Tracking Antenna

2026-05-23 · drone-relay-beam-tracking

Overview

A lightweight add-on module for a relay drone that receives FPV video at 5.8 GHz and retransmits it at 1.3 GHz to a ground station up to 1 km away. The relay drone hovers 100–300 m above the FPV drone. The key capability is RSSI-based beam tracking: a PTZ-mounted patch antenna actively steers toward the FPV drone below, maximising received signal strength without GPS and without any modification to the FPV drone.

Antenna diversity is maintained throughout: an omnidirectional lollypop antenna stays active at all times as a fallback, switching automatically during acquisition scans and signal-loss events. The patch antenna is the primary receiver once lock is achieved.

Tracking Algorithm

Phase 1 — Acquisition (scan)

  1. Lollypop omni is active; establish baseline RSSI.
  2. Pan servo sweeps 270° in 10° steps (~28 positions). Hold 30 ms per step for VRX AGC to settle. Record RSSI.
  3. Identify peak RSSI azimuth → fine sweep ±20° around it in 2° steps.
  4. Tilt sweep: start at –15°, probe ±10° in 2° steps to find peak elevation.
  5. Lock onto peak (azimuth, elevation) in world frame. Switch RX to patch. Total time: ~3–5 s.

Phase 2 — Track (dither hill-climbing, 100 ms loop)

  1. Sample RSSI at current position; maintain rolling 5-sample average to reduce noise.
  2. Pan: probe +3° and –3°; move toward higher RSSI; hold if neither improves.
  3. Tilt: probe +2° and –2°; same logic.
  4. Apply IMU compensation every iteration: servo_cmd = world_target − drone_yaw / drone_pitch.

Phase 3 — Loss recovery

  1. RSSI below threshold for >500 ms → switch RX back to lollypop (signal maintained).
  2. Trigger new acquisition scan from current servo position outward.
  3. On re-lock, switch RX to patch and resume tracking.

IMU Attitude Compensation

  • MPU-6050 reads relay drone yaw, pitch, roll at 100 Hz via I2C.
  • Pan servo: world_azimuth − drone_yaw → servo command.
  • Tilt servo: world_elevation − drone_pitch → servo command.
  • Keeps patch on target even as relay drone drifts, rotates, or responds to wind.

Angular Speed

The KST DS215MG servo reaches approximately 700°/s unloaded; a conservative 350°/s under load. Required tracking rates are far below this ceiling:

~350°/sservo (loaded)
~2°/sneeded @ 1 km
~38°/sneeded @ 50 m
9×speed margin

Speed is not the limiting factor. RSSI noise and VRX AGC settling time (typically 20–80 ms) determine tracking responsiveness. Validate your specific VRX module's RSSI pin on a scope before committing to a loop rate.

Implementation Flow

  1. Hardware assembly — PTZ bracket & servo mounting small

    3D-print bracket in PETG; mount DS215MG (pan axis) and MG90S (tilt axis); attach 5.8 GHz patch to tilt arm. — no dependencies

    Unknown unknowns. Vibration coupling from rotors into patch; rubber damping mounts may be needed. Limitations. Pan hard-limited to 270°; relay drone yaw >135° from centre exceeds servo range.
  2. ESP32 firmware — RSSI ADC + servo PWM medium

    Read RSSI from VRX analog pin via ESP32 ADC (12-bit). Drive pan/tilt via PWM. Validate RSSI linearity across signal levels. — depends on step 1

    Unknown unknowns. RSSI pin behaviour varies by VRX module — bench-test your specific receiver first. Limitations. ESP32 ADC non-linearity near rails; add voltage divider to keep RSSI in 0.1–3.1 V range.
  3. IMU integration — MPU-6050 attitude correction medium

    Mount MPU-6050 rigidly to relay drone frame (not to the PTZ). Read yaw/pitch via I2C; apply complementary filter; subtract drone attitude from servo commands. — depends on step 2

    Unknown unknowns. Gyro yaw drifts ~0.5°/min without magnetometer; acceptable for typical FPV flight durations. Limitations. Magnetic interference from motors and ESCs may corrupt magnetometer readings if one is added later.
  4. Acquisition algorithm — coarse + fine scan medium

    270° coarse pan sweep, fine ±20° sweep, tilt sweep. Output: locked (azimuth, elevation) in world frame. — depends on steps 2 and 3

    Unknown unknowns. Multipath can create secondary RSSI peaks; use peak-width as a confidence score to reject false candidates. Limitations. Acquisition takes 3–5 s; video quality during scan relies on lollypop fallback.
  5. Track loop — dither hill-climbing medium

    100 ms control loop: dither pan ±3°, tilt ±2°, move toward RSSI gradient. Rolling 5-sample average for noise rejection. — depends on step 4

    Unknown unknowns. Optimal dither step size depends on patch beamwidth — tune in the field. Limitations. If VRX AGC settles slower than 50 ms, effective loop rate must be reduced.
  6. Loss recovery + antenna switching small

    RSSI threshold detection; switch RX to lollypop on loss via RF switch; re-trigger acquisition. — depends on step 5

    Limitations. Binary switching only — no true diversity combining. Video may drop 1–2 frames on each antenna switch.
  7. Field tuning — thresholds, noise floor, dither size large

    Fly FPV drone at various ranges and angles. Tune RSSI loss threshold, dither step size, scan speed, AGC settle delay. Log RSSI and servo positions for analysis. — depends on all prior steps

    Unknown unknowns. Real-world multipath, prop wash on relay drone, EMI from 1.3 GHz VTX to RSSI circuit — environment-dependent. Limitations. RSSI-only tracking cannot distinguish multipath from the true signal; some false-tracking in cluttered environments is expected.

Risks & Open Questions

  • Analog RSSI noise. Multipath and fast fading can create false peaks. Use peak-width as a confidence score during acquisition; smooth the tracking loop with a rolling average.
  • Pan range hard limit. 270° servo covers ±135° yaw. If the relay drone rotates more than this from the initial heading, the patch will lose track. The IMU can detect and flag this condition.
  • IMU yaw drift. MPU-6050 gyro integration drifts ~0.5°/min without a magnetometer. Acceptable for typical 10–20 minute flights; add a magnetometer for long hover operations.
  • Near-vertical geometry. FPV drone directly below relay (<50 m lateral) pushes tilt toward –90° where patch gain degrades. Lollypop fallback handles this case.
  • EMI from 1.3 GHz VTX. The transmitter and 5.8 GHz RX share the same platform. Physical separation and shielding of the RSSI circuit are essential.
  • VRX AGC latency. If the receiver AGC settles slower than 50 ms, dither-based tracking will lag. Validate on a bench before tuning the control loop.
  • Vibration. Rotor vibration couples into the IMU and the patch; rubber damping mounts recommended for both.
